Thai Pronunciation Guide for English Speakers

Thai pronunciation differs from English in four big ways: (1) many consonants come in aspirated vs. unaspirated pairs, (2) vowel length (short vs. long) changes meaning, (3) several final consonants are unreleased, and (4) Thai is a tone language with five tones. The tables below give the most useful contrasts first.

Thai Sound / Feature IPA Example (Thai) Closest English Cue
Unaspirated p vs. Aspirated ph p ~ pʰ ปา [paː] vs. พา [pʰaː] English p at word-start is usually aspirated [pʰ]; Thai contrasts both—feel the puff of air.
Unaspirated t vs. Aspirated th t ~ tʰ ตา [taː] vs. ทา [tʰaː] Not English “th” in thin. It’s plain t with/without a strong puff.
Unaspirated k vs. Aspirated kh k ~ kʰ กา [kaː] vs. คา [kʰaː] Like English k vs. a strongly puffed k.
จ, ช (affricates) tɕ ~ tɕʰ จะ [tɕaʔ] vs. ชา [tɕʰaː] Like “ch” in chew, but with a clear aspirated vs. unaspirated pair.
ร (r) and ล (l) r ~ l เรา [raw] , ลา [laː] ร is a tapped/trilled r (not English ɹ). Casual speech may blur ร↔ล.
ย (y) and ว (w) j ~ w ยา [jaː] , ว่า [wâː] (tone varies by spelling) Like English y in yes and w in we.
Vowel length matters a ~ aː ; i ~ iː ; u ~ uː … ปัะ [paʔ] vs. ปา [paː] Short vs. long vowels change meaning. Long vowels are held clearly longer (marked ː in IPA).
Core vowels i, iː ; e, eː ; ɛ, ɛː ; ɤ, ɤː ; ə (unstressed); o, oː ; ɔ, ɔː ; u, uː ; a, aː ดี [diː], เด [deː], แด [dɛː], เดอ [dɤː], โด [doː], ดอ [dɔː], ดู [duː], ดา [daː] Learn pairs (short~long). ɤː is like stressed er in fern (but without English r).
Common diphthongs aj, aw, ia, ɯa, ua ไม่ [mâj], ดาว [daːw], เคียว [kʰiaw], เมื่อ [mɯ̂a], ตัว [tuːa] aj ≈ “eye”; aw ≈ “ow”; ia/ɯa/ua glide smoothly—no extra syllable.
Final stops are unreleased p̚, t̚, k̚ กับ [kap̚], จัด [tɕat̚], นัก [nak̚] Cut the air and stop—no audible “burst.” Never say final b, d, g.
Other final consonants m, n, ŋ, j, w ทำ [tʰam], ทน [tʰon], ดัง [daŋ], ใบ [baj], ดาว [daːw] As in English, but keep them short and crisp at word-end.
Short open syllables end with a catch ʔ (glottal stop) จะ [tɕaʔ] Like the catch in “uh-oh.” Very brief, not a full extra syllable.

Thai Tones (Overview)

Thai has five lexical tones. Tone choice changes meaning. The exact tone depends on consonant class (mid / high / low), tone mark, and whether the syllable is live (ends in a vowel, m n ŋ j w) or dead (ends in a short vowel or p̚ t̚ k̚). For a practical start, learn the five contours and a simple mark mapping for mid-class consonants in live syllables.

Tone Name Contour (arrows) Phonetic Level Starter Rule (Mid-class, live syllable)
Mid → ˧ No tone mark
Low ↘ (low) ˩ ่ (mai èk)
Falling ˥↘ ˥˩ ้ (mai tho)
High ↗ (high) ˦˥ ๊ (mai tri)
Rising ˩↗ ˩˥ ๋ (mai chattawa)

Important: The table above is a starter mapping for mid-class consonants (ก จ ด ต บ ป อ etc.) in live syllables. With high/low consonant classes or dead syllables, the surface tone changes. Learn tones by ear early.

Additional Symbols and Practical Rules

- Long vowels are marked with ː in IPA: มา [maː] vs. มะ [maʔ].
- Aspirated stops are written with an h (ph, th, kh) in romanization: พ [pʰ], ท [tʰ], ค [kʰ].
- Final p t k are unreleased: [p̚ t̚ k̚]; never pronounce final b d g in Thai.
- A very short open syllable often ends with a brief glottal stop [ʔ]: จะ [tɕaʔ].
- Thai ร is a tap/trill [r]; English r [ɹ] will be understood but is not the target.
- Spelling shows tone marks (่ ้ ๊ ๋), but final tone depends on consonant class and syllable type—listen and imitate.
